# Chicken Care Knowledge Base

## What is Safe to Feed Chickens
Chickens can safely eat a variety of foods in addition to their main feed.

Safe treats include:
- Leafy greens (lettuce, spinach, kale)
- Vegetables (carrots, cucumbers, squash)
- Fruits (berries, apples without seeds, melon)
- Mealworms and insects
- Grains (corn, oats in moderation)

Treats should only make up a small portion of the diet.

---

## What Foods Are Unsafe for Chickens
Some foods are harmful to chickens and should be avoided:

- Chocolate
- Avocado (especially skin and pit)
- Raw dried beans
- Moldy or spoiled food
- Highly salty or processed foods
- Sugary foods

These foods can cause illness or toxicity.

---

## What Should Chickens Eat Daily
Chickens should mainly eat a complete commercial feed.

Typical feeding plan:
- Chicks: starter feed
- Growing chickens: grower feed
- Laying hens: layer feed with calcium

Always provide:
- Clean fresh water
- Grit if eating anything besides feed

---

## Feed Stages by Age
Chicken diets should change as they grow:

- Starter feed (young chicks)
- Grower/developer feed (before laying age)
- Layer feed (once egg production begins)

Non-laying chickens should not stay on layer feed long-term due to excess calcium.

---

## Chick Feed Basics
Chicks must be fed a proper starter feed designed for growth.

Avoid using substitutes like:
- Oatmeal
- Grain scraps
- Kitchen leftovers

Starter feed provides the correct balance of protein, vitamins, and minerals.

---

## Medicated vs. Non-Medicated Starter Feed
Chick starter may be medicated or non-medicated.

- Medicated feed can help prevent coccidiosis
- Non-medicated feed is typically used if chicks are already vaccinated

Choose feed based on your flock’s health plan.

---

## Water and Feeder Setup for Chicks
Young chicks need safe and accessible food and water.

Guidelines:
- Use shallow waterers to prevent drowning
- Bright-colored feeders may help chicks find food
- Ensure enough space so all chicks can eat

---

## Buying Chicks Safely
When buying chicks, choose a reputable hatchery, breeder, or flock source.

Some hatcheries offer optional vaccinations for chicks, including protection against coccidiosis and Marek’s disease.

---

## Pullets vs. Day-Old Chicks
Day-old chicks require more care, including temperature control and frequent monitoring.

Pullets are young hens (15–22 weeks old) and are easier for beginners since they are close to laying age.

---

## Brooder Setup for Chicks
Young chicks need a warm, draft-free brooder.

Basic setup:
- Use a chick guard about 24 inches high
- Avoid square corners where chicks may crowd
- Provide about 0.5 square feet per chick (up to 6 weeks)
- Keep chicks separate from older birds

---

## Chick Temperature Needs
Chicks cannot regulate body temperature well.

Guidelines:
- Start at 95°F during week 1
- Reduce by 5°F each week
- Fully feathered by ~6 weeks

Behavior clues:
- Huddling = too cold
- Spreading out = too hot

---

## Heat Sources for Chicks
Common heat sources:
- Heat lamps (secure carefully to prevent fire)
- Brooder heat plates
- Temperature-controlled heaters

Heat plates are often safer than heat lamps.

---

## Cleaning Before New Chicks Arrive
Always clean and disinfect equipment before introducing new chicks.

Clean:
- Brooder or coop surfaces
- Feeders and waterers
- Nesting areas and perches

---

## Manure and Ammonia Control
Waste buildup can create unhealthy conditions.

- Remove wet litter regularly
- Keep bedding dry
- Poor ventilation leads to ammonia buildup
- Ammonia can harm chickens and humans

---

## Composting and Waste Management
Chicken manure can be composted and used as fertilizer.

Follow local guidelines for:
- Waste disposal
- Dead bird handling
- Composting practices

---

## Human Health and Safety
Chickens can carry germs such as:
- Salmonella
- Campylobacter
- E. coli

Safety practices:
- Wash hands after handling chickens
- Supervise children
- Clean scratches or pecks immediately

---

## Chicken Lifespan
Chickens typically live 5–10 years, with some reaching up to 15 years.

Egg production:
- Highest during first 1–2 years
- Declines with age

Factors affecting lifespan:
- Diet and nutrition
- Living conditions
- Disease prevention
- Stress levels
- Predator protection

---

## Marek's Disease
Marek's disease is a viral disease that affects chickens.

Common symptoms:
- Weakness or lethargy
- Paralysis of legs or wings
- Difficulty walking
- Weight loss
- Vision problems or eye changes

Important details:
- It affects the nervous system
- Can cause tumors and immune suppression
- Most common in young chickens

Treatment:
- There is no cure
- Provide supportive care (food, water, comfort)
- Isolate infected birds

Prevention:
- Vaccination is the best protection

---

## Poultry Red Mites
Poultry red mites are small parasites that feed on chickens, usually at night.

Common signs:
- Irritated or restless chickens
- Pale combs (from blood loss)
- Drop in egg production
- Mites hiding in coop cracks

Treatment:
- Clean and disinfect the coop
- Replace bedding
- Use poultry-safe mite treatments
- Repeat treatment as needed

---

## Coccidiosis
Coccidiosis is a common intestinal disease in young chickens.

Common symptoms:
- Bloody or watery droppings
- Weakness
- Loss of appetite
- Puffing up
- Weight loss

Treatment:
- Use medicated feed or treatment as directed
- Keep water clean and available
- Maintain dry bedding

Prevention:
- Use medicated starter feed if needed
- Keep living area clean

---

## Worms in Chickens
Chickens can develop internal parasites (worms).

Common signs:
- Weight loss
- Diarrhea
- Weakness
- Reduced egg production

Treatment:
- Use deworming medication when needed
- Follow proper dosing instructions

Prevention:
- Keep coop clean
- Rotate grazing areas if possible

---

## When to Call a Veterinarian
Seek professional help if chickens show:
- Severe weakness or paralysis
- Not eating or drinking
- Bloody droppings
- Rapid weight loss
- Difficulty breathing

Early action improves chances of recovery.
